Esophageal Cancer (EC) Community at Smart Patients on ACOR

Background

The EC-Group at ACOR started in 1996. In June, 2013, EC-Group moved from ACOR to the Esophageal Cancer Community at Smart Patients.

Smart Patients is an online community for motivated patients and their families and friends. In the Esophageal Cancer Community you can learn at your own level about scientific developments related to your condition, share your questions and concerns with other members, and use what you learn in the context of your own life

Wikipedia

If you or a loved one suffer from a rare condition you should not consider any wikipedia article to be an inclusive source of information regarding that condition. Due to the strict editing guidelines of wikipedia, some of the most useful sources of hard to find information regarding your condition, including patient communities like Smart Patients, cannot be mentioned in any article. In conclusion, wikipedia articles should be neither the first nor the principal source of information to give to a newly diagnosed patient, since wikipedia doesn't accept, in principle, mention of any patient-generated information because that information is not considered "authoritative".

— Gilles Frydman, One of Smart Patients Founders

Using Smart Patients

In order to get the most benefit from Smart Patients the most important thing is to participate both asking and answering questions.

Be sure to check the tutorials, videos and frequently asked questions. The Smart Patients team is very willing to help with using the site.

Is an Online Community for You?

There is a need for on-line support for people with EC as well as their caregivers, because there are not a lot of other sources for information. New treatments are being developed and people are surviving longer. There is a growing community of patients and caregivers who look to each other for tips, ideas and information that they are not always able to get from their doctors.

The community has a fund of knowledge about the latest advances in treatment; new chemotherapy agents; clinical trials; or innovative procedures.

Community members trade hints and tips for all those little things that the medical professionals do not tell you. Things like what kinds of foods to avoid/try during chemo; how to manage hygiene despite a 24-hour chemo pump; how to keep a j-tube from clogging; what to expect after surgery; how to cope with ?chemo-brain?; or how to navigate the insurance maze.

The scarcity of information from professional health care providers is especially pronounced during the long-term survival phase. The community members who have made it long enough to be discharged by their doctors as needing no further follow-up usually still have a number of ?late effects? to deal with, and there is usually no textbook nor any ?expert? to turn to for help. Instead we help each other to help ourselves. We, as a community, are still writing the book on this subject, via groups and sites like Smart Patients.

They say it takes a village to raise a child --- It also takes a village to nurse a sick person. For EC, the on-line community is that village.
